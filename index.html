Advanced Autonomous Farming

Vision-sensing technology expertly integrated into Amos' operating system allows for autonomous farming without the need to constantly observe the machine at work.  For example, Amos notifies the operator if it is confronted by an obstruction in its path.  The operator then chooses the option for Amos to continue on its way or to wait for the removal of the obstruction.  Because Amos can watch out for itself, the farmer is free to multitask, increasing their efficiency.

Amos Modular
Design

Amos' modular design provides unmatched serviceability across the platform.  Each major component, such as batteries, coolant package, and track assembly, can be removed independently of the others.  Without the need to remove multiple components for the service of just one, the outcome is more efficient servicing.  By allowing Amos and the farmer to spend more time in the field and less time in the shop, profitability is optimized..

Omni-scalable
Power Source

Designed to simultaneously operate multiple machines all on a platform that is completely interchangeable.  Regardless which Amos models the farmer is operating, they will all connect with each other seamlessly.  No need for the operator to switch between platforms to operate equipment nor the hours of training on multiple systems.

The Plan

Vision:

AMOS is a design build project which takes aim at the engineering challenges of fitting tractor-quality power and capability onto a relatively small frame.

For this initial build, our team focused on engineering Amos as an electric vehicle capable of inter-seeding cover crop within corn fields, where the machinery must be small enough to navigate a 30-inch gap between the rows of crop without causing damage.

Additionally, it must possess the ability to deliver enough power to haul and distribute the inter-seeding.

Advanced Farming:

Designing and building a small, modular vehicle powerful enough to pull large loads along precise routes proved to be a significant engineering challenge. As with any design, the team regularly met stark realities when taking the computer-generated model to manufacture.

Tolerances quickly stacked up, making it necessary to rely heavily on the strong relationships that exist between the prototyping, engineering, and tooling professionals within our organization in order to complete the mission.

The exercise of designing and engineering Amos as an Omni-Scalable build sets the stage for the future of utilizing the technology across multiple platforms.
